Background

Sixty-two caskets, 13,000 bones, and one massive puzzle have given military scientists the challenge of their lives: reassembling the jumbled are mains into matched sets, comparing them to DNA of living relatives and returning them for burial. The 100th identification came just ahead of the 76th anniversary of Japanâ€™s attack on Pearl Harbor, and the Defense POW/MIA Accounting Agency has since identified at least two more. Since 2015 Carrie Brown and her team been working to identify unidentified remains of World War II seamen who served on the USS Oklahoma so they can be returned to long-waiting widows, children, siblings and, in many cases, nieces and nephews they never knew.

The data analysis part is crucial in identifying the bones and for this we would be using graph theoretic algorithms bipartite matching where different attributes of skeletal bone can be used to arrive upon a perfect matching or a near-perfect matching. The other algorithm that we would be employing is the clustering algorithm which would help us in clustering skeletal bones with similar attributes together.
